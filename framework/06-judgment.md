# Judgment framework

How decisions get made and recorded so they're consistent across roles and not relitigated.

## Decide, then record the *why*

A decision without its reasoning gets re-argued every time someone new looks at it. Every non-trivial
decision is captured as: **what was decided + why + the date** (see the decisions KB and the resolution
log). The reasoning is the durable part.

## A judgment axis (per domain)

Each domain benefits from an explicit, short list of the questions it repeatedly weighs — its
**judgment axis**. Instead of re-deriving criteria each time, you score the situation against the axis.
A generic axis to adapt:

1. **Scope** — is this inside what we own, or is it a separate effort?
2. **Reversibility** — can we undo it cheaply? (If yes, we can be decisive; if no, slow down.)
3. **Cost vs payoff** — does the effort/context-cost match the value?
4. **Asset impact** — does it risk the things we must protect (working rules, tooling, trust)?
5. **Confidence** — do we actually *know*, or are we assuming? (If assuming, verify first.)
6. **Boundary** — does it cross a confidentiality / authority boundary we must respect?

Domains add their own axis items (e.g. a client-facing domain adds "client-request fit"; a
quality-gated domain adds "passes the quality bar"). Keep the list short enough to actually use.

## The "decisive vs cautious" rule

- **Reversible** action + clear case → act decisively (reversibility is the safety net).
- **Irreversible** action, or genuinely ambiguous → slow down, confirm, default to no-change.

This is what keeps "be safe" from collapsing into "do nothing," and keeps "be decisive" from becoming
reckless. The dividing line is *reversibility*, not gut feeling.

## Failure and withdrawal judgment

Owning an outcome includes the duty to call it when it isn't working. A lead who sees a sustained miss
proposes the pivot or the stop themselves — rather than letting a losing effort coast. Recording the
withdrawal (and why) is as important as recording a launch.
